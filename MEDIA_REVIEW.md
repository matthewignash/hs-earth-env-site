### Three Degrees Warmer — Dhaka segment (The Economist)

- **URL**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uynhvHZUOOo
- **Subject**: Dhaka (Bangladesh) at 3°C of warming. Bay of Bengal megacity case study.
- **Verified content**: NOT YET — confirm via direct watch. The footer of the YouTube page hints at The Economist as publisher; the user confirmed "this is Dhaka, not Chennai." Need length, publication date, and specific scenes verified before linking.
- **Best fit in U4**:
  - **Block 7 (Regional Climate + Lock Challenge)** — primary. The global→regional translation block. Dhaka is a regional peer (Bay of Bengal coastal megacity, monsoon-dependent, low-elevation, dense urban population). Students see the same climate scenarios playing out in a peer city, then translate to Chennai. Exactly the K/U→T/T move the block is built around.
  - **Block 4 (Human Acceleration + Warming Signal)** — possible secondary. If the clip emphasises visible warming-signal evidence (sea-level, salinity intrusion, cyclone intensity), it anchors the "warming signal is real, measurable, and already affecting Bay of Bengal cities" message.
  - **Block 12 (Policy Panel + Unit Close)** — possible unit-close use. "Here's a city in the same situation; what do you take from this into your life beyond the course?"
- **NOT a fit for**: Block 1 (would muddy the Chennai-specific bell ringer) or Block 5 (would muddy the assessment kickoff, which needs Chennai-specific framing).
- **Teaching move it enables**: *source-applicability evaluation*. A real research skill — just because two cities are climate-peers doesn't mean evidence transfers 1:1. Dhaka is much further inland + lower-elevation than Chennai; the documentary's specific numbers must NOT be treated as Chennai's. This OPVL Limitation move is sharper than what a Chennai-specific source would teach.
- **OPVL frame for students**:
  - Origin: The Economist (news organisation, UK-based, international audience). NOT a peer-reviewed scientific body.
  - Purpose: Convince Western readers that climate change is real and urgent, using Dhaka as a case study. Journalism + advocacy.
  - Value: Visual + emotional anchor for South Asian climate vulnerability. Accessible to non-experts.
  - Limitations: Selective framing. Possible dramatization. No peer review. Publication date matters — if pre-IPCC AR6 (2021/2023), uses older projections. Most importantly: *this is Dhaka, not Chennai.*
- **Concerns to address before linking**:
  1. **Doom-narrative risk.** U4's posture is agency-mode. Bookend the clip with "what are the people in the clip *doing*?" — keep agency in the room.
  2. **Currency.** If pre-AR6, frame the projections as "what was thought then" vs. "what AR6 says now."
  3. **EAL accessibility.** Confirm captioning quality.
  4. **Length.** Aim for ≤6 min of clip if it's from a longer documentary. Embed a specific time-range.
- **Implementation when confirmed**: Update Block 7's first Watch entry-card from placeholder to real link with duration + OPVL caveat. Add a "regional-peer documentary" line to the Chennai Climate Brief handout's Sources section.

### Sea of Plastic — Chennai's plastic crisis (Down to Earth)

- **URL**: https://www.downtoearth.org.in/waste/sea-of-plastic-chennais-plastic-crisis-fight-to-stop-marine-litter
- **Title**: "Sea of plastic: Chennai's plastic crisis & fight to stop marine litter"
- **Publisher**: Down to Earth — flagship publication of the Centre for Science and Environment (CSE), one of India's most respected environmental policy organisations (Sunita Narain's outlet). Indian framing, Indian audience.
- **Date**: May 12, 2026 — current for AY 26-27.
- **Format**: Multimedia docuseries-article hybrid. The article is built around a documentary film covering the "We Segregate" citizen initiative + "Urban Ocean program" with the Kasturba Nagar Residents Association.
- **Quantitative anchor**: "Chennai generates over 6,000 tonnes of waste daily" — plastic leakage into Adyar, Cooum, Buckingham Canal, stormwater drains → Bay of Bengal.
- **Framing question (real policy debate)**: *"Should the responsibility of tackling plastic waste fall only on citizens, or must packaging companies and manufacturers be held accountable too?"* This is Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) — a real ongoing Indian policy debate. Direct prep for U5 Position Paper work.
- **Verified content**: Title, date, publisher, format, framing question all confirmed via WebFetch. Need to actually read the full article + watch embedded documentary to confirm specific evidence + interventions + scenes before treating any claim as cite-able.
- **Best fit**:
  - **U2 Block 3 (Rivers as Landscape Sculptors)** — strongest fit. Block's central activity is OPVL on three contested sources about the Cooum (government report / news article / AI summary). This article is exactly the journalism-source archetype. With the embedded documentary, students OPVL the multimedia package as one composite source.
  - **U2 Block 4 (Coasts)** — Marine plastic accumulation. Article discusses where waste enters Bay of Bengal.
  - **U2 Landscape Reading assessment Sources section** — strong recommended source for students picking Cooum, Adyar, Marina, Buckingham Canal, or Pulicat as their location.
  - **Future U5 (Hydrosphere) when built** — water-system pollution, EPR policy debate.
  - **Future U6 (Biosphere) when built** — ecosystem impact + citizen-action framing.
- **Teaching move it enables**: Source-type diversity. Counterpoint in source type (multimedia-article hybrid), geography (Chennai-specific Indian), audience (Indian environmental policy readers), and framing (citizen-action + EPR debate) to Western-published sources. The framing question primes U5's Position Paper work.
- **OPVL frame for students**:
  - Origin: CSE / Down to Earth. Indian environmental research + policy publication. Editorial position is broadly pro-environmental-regulation + pro-citizen-action.
  - Purpose: Investigative environmental journalism + advocacy. Document Chennai plastic crisis + showcase citizen-led responses + raise EPR question.
  - Value: Quantitative anchor (6,000 tonnes/day). Geographic specificity (named rivers + drains). Documentary footage of citizen interventions. Frames the EPR policy question explicitly.
  - Limitations: CSE has a known editorial position — students must distinguish reporting from advocacy. Multimedia docuseries format means selective scenes (the dramatic ones make the cut). May undercount packaging-industry counter-arguments. Date matters: post-2024 changes to India's Plastic Waste Management Rules may or may not be reflected.
- **Concerns to address before linking**:
  1. **Editorial position.** CSE is well-known but not neutral. Frame the OPVL Limitation explicitly: this is environmental advocacy journalism, not balanced reporting.
  2. **Length / scope of embedded documentary.** Need to verify what the doc covers (length, scenes, named interventions) before students cite specific claims.
  3. **EPR policy currency.** India's plastic-waste regulations have evolved fast. Verify the article's policy framing is current.
- **Implementation when confirmed**:
  - Update U2 Block 3's three-sources OPVL activity to optionally use this article as the news-article source (replacing the TBD placeholder).
  - Add to U2 Block 4's Read entry-card as a recommended Chennai-coast pollution source.

## Comparison: Dhaka vs. Urban Heatscapes

These two documentaries are **complementary, not competing**. They teach different OPVL moves on different parts of the unit:

| Criterion | Dhaka (Three Degrees) | Chennai (Urban Heatscapes E1) |
|---|---|---|
| Subject | Regional peer city | Students' actual city |
| Teaching move | Source-applicability evaluation | Direct evidence-gathering on a challenge |
| Best fit | Block 7 (global→regional translation) | Block 9 or 10 (adaptation + worked example) |
| OPVL emphasis | "This is NOT Chennai" Limitation | "Documentary vs. peer-reviewed data" Limitation |
| Risk | Doom narrative + currency | Quality of recommendations + selective framing |

If both are confirmed, the U4 deep build can integrate them as complementary Watch entry-cards across Blocks 7, 9, and 10.

## Notes on the OPVL move for documentary sources

Documentary sources are a source type students haven't OPVL'd since U0. By U4, they've practiced OPVL extensively on news articles, government reports, scientific papers, AI outputs, and historical maps — but not on documentary footage. Adding one documentary integration with explicit OPVL framing fills that gap and sharpens the source-evaluation muscle before U5 (where stakeholder voices in the Position Paper work introduce another source category — direct testimony).
